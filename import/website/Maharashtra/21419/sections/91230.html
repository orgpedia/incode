{"footnote":"<\/br>\t\t","content":"<\/span>(1) If a single act or series of acts is\r\nof such a nature that it is doubtful which of several offences the facts which can be proved will constitute,\r\nthe accused may be charged with having committed all or any of such offences, and any number of such\r\ncharges may be tried at once; or he may be charged in the alternative with having committed someone of\r\nthe said offences.<\/br>
\r\n<\/span>(2) If in such a case the accused is charged with one offence, and it appears in evidence that he\r\ncommitted a different offence for which he might have been charged under the provisions of\r\nsub-section (1), he may be convicted of the offence which he is shown to have committed, although he was\r\nnot charged with it.<\/br>
\r\n
Illustrations.<\/i><\/center><\/br>
\r\n<\/span>(a) A is accused of an act which may amount to theft, or receiving stolen property, or criminal breach\r\nof trust or cheating. He may be charged with theft, receiving stolen property, criminal breach of trust and\r\ncheating, or he may be charged with having committed theft, or receiving stolen property, or criminal breach\r\nof trust or cheating.<\/br>
\r\n<\/span>(b) In the case mentioned, A is only charged with theft. It appears that he committed the offence of\r\ncriminal breach of trust, or that of receiving stolen goods. He may be convicted of criminal breach of trust\r\nor of receiving stolen goods (as the case may be), though he was not charged with such offence.<\/br>
\r\n<\/span>(c) A states on oath before the Magistrate that he saw B hit C with a club. Before the Sessions Court A\r\nstates on oath that B never hit C. A may be charged in the alternative and convicted of intentionally giving\r\nfalse evidence, although it cannot be proved which of these contradictory statements was false.<\/br>
